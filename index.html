Robotic manipulation through learning from demonstration (LfD) holds the promise of enabling robots to acquire various actions efficiently. However, existing robotics imitation learning methods typically rely on structured observation-action pairs, requiring costly and laborious data collection, and struggle to generalize manipulation skills from unstructured, real-world videos. To address these limitations, we propose a novel ``Human-to-Robot'' imitation learning pipeline that enables robots to acquire manipulation skills directly from unstructured video demonstrations, inspired by the human ability to learn by ``\textit{watching}'' and ``\textit{imitating}''. Our key innovation is a modular framework that decouples the learning process into two distinct stages: (1) Understanding the demonstration through video, and (2) Robot Immitation to learn the demonstrated manipulations. In the first stage, we use a combination of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Vision-Language Models (VLMs) to extract features related to essential commonsense properties, generating structured, caption-like commands by separately identifying interacted objects and actions. In the second stage, these structured commands guide a robot learning system that is built upon a Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) algorithm to master the demonstrated task. We validated our approach in PyBullet simulation environments, demonstrating that our UR5 robotic arm can robustly and adaptively perform a diverse set of manipulation tasks learned directly from video. Our results show marked superiority over competitive baselines, achieving $85.9\%$ success rate on standard object sets and a 71.3% success rate on novel objects, indicating significantly improved generalization.
